Cricket Guru

A cricket question-answering agent grounded in structured ball-by-ball match data and encyclopedic prose, so it answers both "who scored the most in IPL 2016" and "why the 2019 final was controversial" without making either up.

Group: IP8, Cricket Guru  |  Primary framework: Pydantic AI  |  Domain: Cricket — International men's (Test / ODI / T20I) + IPL

Problem statement

Cricket knowledge lives in two incompatible shapes, and no single retrieval method serves both. Prose holds narrative and judgement: why a match mattered, how a technique worked, what made a decision contentious. Structured records hold exact facts: run tallies, strike rates, results. Ask a vector search for the most runs in a season and it returns a paragraph that sounds right and is often wrong; ask a stats database why a chase was famous and it has no column for that. A third failure sits on top: records go stale the moment they break, so a retrieval-only system answers record questions confidently and out of date.

Cricket Guru reads each question, decides which source actually holds the answer, and pulls from it: exact numbers from a structured match DB, narrative from a text index, and a web-search freshness check for records. The scope for now: men's international cricket and the IPL, factual and narrative questions with checkable answers.

The kinds of questions it must separate:
• Fact: "Who scored the most runs in IPL 2016?" → structured match data.
• Narrative: "Why was the 2019 World Cup final decided on boundary count?" → prose.
• Record / stale: "Who holds the record for most sixes in men's T20Is?" → structured data + web freshness check, not a years-old article.

Comparative evaluation

Ground truth is found and computed, not hand-authored, on two tracks: stats questions templated from Cricsheet with SQL-computed answers (exact match); narrative questions from Sports SE accepted answers, scored by LLM-as-judge and validated against human labels.

We do not just reach for the optimal design. At each pipeline leg we compare two prominent approaches, varying one leg at a time against a simple baseline, so each result shows what the advanced technique actually buys:

  • Chunking: fixed-size vs structural  •  Retrieval: dense vs hybrid (dense+BM25)
  • Routing: rule/keyword router vs LLM tool-calling agent  •  Judge: same-model vs cross-model (self-preference bias)

Reported per variant: accuracy delta, plus hallucination/groundedness, routing accuracy, cost/latency, and web-fallback rate. Per-question logs feed the failure analysis.

Interface

A Streamlit app with two views: a chat (answer + tool trace + sources + corpus-vs-web transparency) and a comparisons dashboard that shows the leg-by-leg results and metrics intuitively.
